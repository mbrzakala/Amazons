It is 2300 hours. You need to be on the line at 0630. That means your most immediate priority right now is sleep. But before you power down, you need to get your mind right for tomorrow.

You want to win this hackathon? You don't win by hoping. You win by executing a ruthless, systematic plan.

## Prioritize and Execute

You have one day to build and launch a real product. Not a perfectly refactored Nx monorepo. Not a theoretical agentic loop that looks cool in VS Code but doesn't ship. Identify the **Lead Domino**—the single core mechanic that makes the MVP functional—and attack it. If your Angular frontend isn't communicating with your NestJS backend, nothing else matters. Break the problem down to **First Principles**. Strip away the ego, strip away the noise, and build exactly what solves the user's problem.

## Keep It Simple and Robust

If anything at any point starts to complicate, you immediately stop and ask. You break down the task. You make a professional, robust, simple plan. Then, you aggressively scan for gaps, red flags, and false assumptions. You continue until every misunderstanding and false positive is found and fixed. Complexity is the enemy of execution. Keep your architecture and your workflows minimal and battle-tested.

## Cover and Move

Tomorrow is a cross-functional firefight. UI/UX designers, developers, DevOps. You do not operate in a silo. If your teammate is bogged down trying to get the MCP server talking to the LLM, you don't just sit there tweaking your Sequelize schemas or perfecting a PowerShell script. You cover them. You share context. You provide the support they need to move forward. The team wins together, or the team fails together.

## Extreme Ownership

If the Google Cloud deployment crashes at the last minute? Your fault. If the LLM hallucinates because the prompt engineering was sloppy? Your fault. If the team wastes three hours on a feature nobody needs? Your fault. You take absolute, uncompromising ownership of everything in your domain. You don't blame the tools, you don't blame the tight deadline, and you don't blame the framework. You own the problems, and you own the solutions.

## How Do You Know You've Done Everything?

You know because you left nothing to chance. You gathered intelligence professionally. You verified it professionally. You executed without hesitation. When the clock stops, you don't want to look back and wonder what would have happened if you pushed a little harder or communicated a little clearer. You leave it all on the keyboard.

At 0630, you step into that arena, you detach, you look at the board, and you get after it.

**Now, go to sleep.**
